# reference/catalyst-library.md — Buy Catalysts

A fleet doesn't buy a safety/retention program because it's big. It buys because something changed and created a reason to act *now*. The catalyst is the difference between a warm account and a cold one. Your job is to find it — or report honestly that there isn't one.

---

## The four catalyst families

### 1. Post-incident
A fatal crash, a hazmat/chemical spill, a high-profile injury, an OSHA citation.
- **Window:** sharp and short — typically **1–2 quarters**. After that the urgency fades and budget re-normalizes.
- **Why it works:** safety leadership is under board and insurer scrutiny and needs to show a concrete initiative.
- **What to verify:** the **date** (an incident "last year" may already be cold), and whether it's in *their* operation vs an owner-operator they can disclaim.
- **Handle with care:** never open a sales conversation by citing someone's fatality crassly. The catalyst sets *timing and posture*; it is not an opening line.

### 2. Labor pressure
A strike, a walkout, a Teamsters action, a publicized turnover spike, a driver shortage in their lane.
- **Window:** medium — months, as long as the pressure persists.
- **Why it works:** retention spend gets approved when drivers are visibly walking out the door.
- **What to verify:** is it active or resolved? A settled strike is a spent catalyst.

### 3. Ownership / leadership change
An acquisition, a PE sponsor with an exit timeline, a new CEO/CFO/CPO/VP-Safety/CHRO.
- **Window:** wide — **6–18 months**, peaking in the first year.
- **Why it works:** **new executives buy to make a mark.** A safety/retention initiative is an easy "here's what I changed in year one" win. This is often the *strongest* catalyst because it's about a person's incentives, not a one-time event.
- **What to verify:** time in seat (under 12 months = highest receptivity), and whether they came from a company that used a program like yours.
- **Special case — PE ownership:** a sponsor mid-hold with a 5–7 year exit clock wants operational-improvement stories before sale. Time your pitch to the hold-period midpoint.

### 4. Regulatory / insurance
A nuclear verdict in their lane, an insurance non-renewal or hard-market renewal, a CSA threshold breach, new FMCSA rulemaking.
- **Window:** tied to the renewal/compliance date — often a hard deadline.
- **Why it works:** a hazmat carrier facing a hard-market renewal has a board-level, dollar-denominated reason to fund a safety initiative.
- **What to verify:** the **renewal month** (this converts "someday" into "next 60 days"), and whether the verdict was actually in their segment.

## How to *detect* each catalyst (where the signal actually shows up)

A catalyst family is useless until you know which data source reveals it. This is the detection map — pair it with `data-sources.md` for the pull mechanics.

| Family | Where the signal surfaces | Tier of the signal | The confirm step |
|--------|---------------------------|--------------------|------------------|
| **Post-incident** | News + **PACER/state docket** + FMCSA crash record + OSHA citations | Existence T1; magnitude/number T3 | Run the full `incident-research.md` playbook — confirm it's *theirs* by USDOT#, date the window, quarantine the dollar figure. |
| **Labor** | NLRB filings, union (Teamsters) site/news, local press, Glassdoor churn chatter | T1–T3 | Confirm **active vs. settled** — a resolved strike is a spent catalyst. |
| **Ownership / leadership** | Press releases, PE-firm portfolio pages, SEC (if public), **LinkedIn tenure** | Announcement T1; "started date" off LinkedIn **T4** | Confirm time-in-seat (<12 mo = hottest) and whether the exec came from a company that used a program like the operator's. |
| **Regulatory / insurance** | **FMCSA SMS** (BASIC drift), **SAFER** (Conditional rating), **L&I** (insurance lapse/non-renewal), rulemaking dockets | BASIC T2; rating/lapse T1 | Get the **renewal month** or the **dated rating** — this converts "someday" into "next 60 days." |

The pattern across all four: **the data points you at the catalyst; you confirm the specific dated event before it's real.** A drifting score is a symptom; the catalyst is the cause you locate and date.

---

## How to use the library

1. For any target, ask: **which family, if any, applies right now?**
2. If you find one: date it, tier it, and check its window is still open.
3. If you find none: **say so.** "No live catalyst — this is a cold account, recommend deprioritize" is a finding, not a failure. The operator's time is the scarce resource; protecting it from catalyst-free accounts is half your value.
4. If you find two (e.g., a spill *and* an insurance renewal): that's a high-priority account — stacked catalysts compound urgency.

---

## The anti-pattern

Do not manufacture a catalyst to make an account look promising. "They're a large carrier so they probably care about safety" is **not** a catalyst — it's a horoscope true of every fleet. A real catalyst is a *dated, specific, recent change*. If you can't point to one, there isn't one.
