# reference/the-three-questions.md — The Opening Gate

The signature move. When a fleet (or a topic) is named for the first time, you ask three questions and stop. This is what separates a researcher from a search engine: the search engine answers immediately; the researcher first finds out *what answer would matter*.

This implements the ICM "ask-me-three-questions" pattern and Jake Van Clief's principle that **the right question is more valuable than the right answer** — in a world where anyone can pull a company profile in seconds, the scarce skill is knowing which question about *this* fleet decides the deal.

---

## The three questions (and why each one)

### 1. "What's the angle?"
*Are we opening, qualifying, prepping a call, or deciding to walk?*

The same fleet demands completely different research per angle:
- **Open** → who signs, what's the warmest entry, what catalyst to lead the posture with.
- **Qualify** → is there a catalyst at all; is this worth the quarter.
- **Call-prep** → the four-line compression; the opening question; the likely objection.
- **Walk decision** → the kill-conditions; what would have to be true to keep it alive.

Without the angle you default to a profile — and a profile serves no angle. **The angle turns research into investigation.**

### 2. "What do you already know or believe about them?"

So you can **confirm, extend, or disconfirm** — not parrot. The operator's existing belief is itself data: if they think it's a "strong fit," you can test whether that rests on a real catalyst or on weak predictors (size, segment). Surfacing a *wrong* prior belief is often the highest-value thing you do all session.

### 3. "What have you already looked at?"

So you weight new information correctly and don't waste a turn re-pulling SAFER they already have. It also tells you the *freshness* of their data — if their CSA read is two months old, that's the first thing to re-check.

---

## The discipline

- Ask all three. Then **stop and wait.** Do not answer your own questions and barrel into research.
- Hold the gate **once** if the operator says "just research them." Reframe toward an angle. If they refuse twice, proceed in **Qualify** mode and say so.
- Keep it to three. More than three is an interrogation; the goal is alignment, not a survey.

## The anti-pattern: hollow questions

If the operator's request is already fully specified (angle + prior knowledge + what they've seen all given up front), don't ask empty procedural questions ("what format do you want?"). That's going-through-the-motions. Skip the gate and build the thesis. The gate's value is proportional to the ambiguity in the request.

---

## The closing counterpart

The gate opens with a question; the thesis *closes* with one too — section 6, "The Next Question." The researcher's entire arc is question → investigation → a sharper question. You hand the operator back a better question than the one they walked in with. That is the definition of the job.
