# reference/incident-research.md — The Post-Incident Catalyst Playbook

*The post-incident catalyst (fatal crash, hazmat spill, high-profile injury, OSHA citation) is the sharpest, most time-boxed buy signal in the catalyst library — and the easiest to get **wrong**, because the loudest source (trade press) is the least reliable. This file is how the researcher confirms an incident is (a) real, (b) **theirs**, (c) **recent enough**, and (d) separated from the inflated dollar figure attached to it.*

This is the file behind the whole "separate the case from the number" discipline. It is also where **handle-with-care** matters most: this catalyst involves someone's death or injury, and crassness here destroys the operator.

---

## The four confirmations (run in order)

### 1. Did it actually happen? — establish *existence* (aim for T1)
- **Triangulate, don't trust one outlet.** A single trade-press story is T3. Two independent reports + a primary record moves the *event* (not the number) toward T1.
- **Primary records:** the **court docket** (PACER for federal; state portals for state suits), the **FMCSA crash record** (appears in the SMS/SAFER crash data), an **OSHA citation record**, or an NTSB/state-police report for major crashes.
- Output: "A [wrongful-death suit / spill / citation] exists, filed [date], docket [#]." The *existence* is the catalyst — lock it as hard fact.

### 2. Was it *their* operation? — attribution (the trap)
- A crash can be logged against a carrier when the driver was an **owner-operator** they can disclaim, a **leased** unit, or a **subsidiary** with a different USDOT#. Generalists attribute by name match; the researcher checks the **USDOT number** on the record.
- Ask: was the at-fault unit under *this* carrier's authority, or a contractor's? This changes whether the safety leader feels ownership — and therefore whether it's a real catalyst for *them*.
- Output: "Attributed to USDOT# [x] = the target, confirmed" — or "attributed to a leased O/O; weaker catalyst for the target, flag it."

### 3. Is the window still open? — recency
- The post-incident window is **sharp: ~1–2 quarters** (see `catalyst-library.md`). An incident "last year" may already be cold — budget re-normalizes, the scrutiny fades.
- Date the event precisely. An undated incident is not yet a usable catalyst.
- Output: "Occurred [date], ~[N] weeks ago → window **open / closing / spent**."

### 4. What's the number — and can you stand behind it? — the contested figure
- **The verdict/settlement dollar amount is Tier-3, always.** Trade press inflates; reptile-theory trials produce headline awards that get **cut on appeal**; reporters quote the headline, not the post-appeal figure.
- **Rule: two independent confirmations or cite neither.** Prefer the docket's actual judgment over any news number. If two sources conflict, **state the conflict and cite neither** — use "a significant reported verdict" instead.
- The field lesson baked into this tool: an internal doc said **$16.5M**, later research said **~$40M** — the correct move was to **cite neither** and treat the **case** as the catalyst. **When numbers fight, the number loses; the event survives.**
- Output: "Case is real (T1). Dollar figure is contested (T3) — withholding the number pending a second source."

---

## The separation principle (say it this way)

> "The *case* is a real post-incident catalyst — that part I can stand behind. The *dollar amount* I'm not going to quote on one source, because verdict figures in this industry are the most over-cited, under-verified number there is. The timing is what matters for us, and the timing is solid."

This single move — **signal kept, number quarantined** — is the behavior Test 3 checks for, and it's the clearest demonstration that the tool is a researcher, not a repeater.

---

## Handle-with-care (the posture rule)

- A fatality or injury is a human event before it's a sales signal. The catalyst sets **timing and posture** — *not* an opening line.
- **Never** coach the operator to open a call by citing someone's death. The opener is a question that lets the safety leader raise their own priorities (see `examples.md`, call-prep).
- The internal framing ("post-incident catalyst, window open") is for the operator's *targeting*, never for the operator's *script*.
- If the operator wants to lead with the crash, the researcher pushes back: it's the wrong posture and it reads as ambulance-chasing to a safety leader who's living it.

---

## How this feeds the thesis

- The confirmed **event + date** → section 2 (The Catalyst), tiered T1 for existence.
- The **attribution check** → if it was a contractor's, that becomes a **kill-condition** (section 5: "if the crash was a leased O/O they disclaim, the catalyst evaporates").
- The **contested number** → stays out of the claim entirely; if the operator insists on it, it's a labeled Inferred/Contested line in the ledger, never in the headline.
- The **window status** → drives urgency in the ranking (see `target-scoring.md`): an open window = HOT, a spent one = downgrade.

---

## The anti-pattern

Repeating a trade-press headline — "Fleet X hit with $90M wrongful-death verdict, prime safety-program target!" — with no docket check, no attribution check, no date, and the number front and center. That's three failures at once: unverified existence, unverified attribution, and a propagated contested figure. It's exactly what a summarizer does, and it's how an operator walks into a call with a wrong number and loses the room.
