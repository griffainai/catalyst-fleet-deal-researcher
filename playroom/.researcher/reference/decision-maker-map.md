# reference/decision-maker-map.md — Who Actually Signs

The operator's most common, most expensive mistake is touching the wrong person first. This is how you map authority at an enterprise trucking fleet for a safety/retention program — **as roles with confidence labels, never as invented names.**

---

## The authority map (for a 500–3,000 unit fleet)

| Role | Function in the deal | Typical authority |
|------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **VP of Safety / Chief Safety Officer** | Owns the problem; usually owns or strongly influences the program budget | **Primary signer** — start here for most fleets |
| **CFO** | Gatekeeper when spend ties to insurance, nuclear-verdict exposure, or is large | **Co-signer / blocker** |
| **VP Operations** | Cares about driver availability and downtime | Influencer; sometimes co-signer at ops-led fleets |
| **CHRO / VP People** | Feels turnover pain; owns retention metrics | **Influencer** — strong champion, rarely holds the budget alone |
| **CEO / President** | At founder-led or family fleets, may be the *single* signer | Variable — high at small/family fleets, low at PE-owned |
| **Procurement** | Process gate on larger contracts | Blocker, not a buyer — never lead here |

---

## Rules for using this map

1. **Target the role, then find the name.** Decide the *role* with confidence (usually VP Safety). Treat the *name* as an Unknown with a fast close — never present a scraped or guessed name as "the buyer."
2. **Confidence-label every name you do find.** "Confirmed (LinkedIn + company page, dated)" vs "Likely (single stale source)" vs "Inferred (pattern only)."
3. **Single-signer fleets are faster.** Family-owned and founder-led fleets often collapse authority to one person — flag this as a speed advantage when you see it.
4. **New-in-seat changes the map.** A VP Safety <12 months in role is both the *signer* and a *catalyst* (see `catalyst-library.md`). Weight heavily.
5. **Match the catalyst to the signer.** Insurance-renewal catalyst → CFO is in the room. Post-incident catalyst → Safety VP is in the room. Labor catalyst → CHRO is in the room. The catalyst tells you who to bring the thesis to.

---

## Ownership structure rewires the map

The same role signs differently depending on who owns the fleet. Identify the ownership type early — it changes who's actually in the room.

| Ownership type | Where authority sits | Speed | What to watch |
|----------------|----------------------|-------|---------------|
| **Family / founder-owned** | Often collapses to **one person** (owner-operator-of-the-business, or a founder-CEO). VP Safety may recommend; the founder signs. | **Fast** — single signer | The founder's personal stake; relationships matter more than process. |
| **PE-owned** | Diffuse — a **portfolio-ops layer** above the fleet plus a CFO watching the exit clock. The VP Safety proposes; finance and the sponsor gate. | Slow, but **catalyst-rich** | Hold-period midpoint = appetite for operational-improvement stories before sale (see `catalyst-library.md`). |
| **Public** | Committee + procurement; CFO and risk/insurance functions weigh in formally. | Slowest | SEC filings give you real financials (T1) — rare in this space. |
| **Owner-operator-heavy (drayage/some TL)** | The "fleet" is partly independent contractors — there may be **no W-2 population to retain**. | N/A for a retention sell | A structural **misfit flag**, not a buyer-map problem (see `segment-playbooks.md`). |

**Rule:** map the ownership type before you map the name. A family fleet's speed advantage and a PE fleet's exit-clock catalyst are both deciding facts the operator needs up front.

---

## The refusal

If you don't have a confirmed name, you say: *"The role is [X]. The name is an Unknown — here's how to close it fast."* You give the search recipe (e.g., LinkedIn filtered to fleet + "safety," cross-check SAFER contact, verify email before sending). You do **not** fabricate a contact. A wrong name on a first touch is worse than no name — it signals the operator didn't do their homework.
